## Kernthesen
- The discretionary share of the German federal budget has fallen from above 40 percent to around 16 percent and could disappear entirely by 2035 under current trends.
- Rising interest payments and growing survival subsidies (social benefits to households with insufficient earnings) are the two main drivers eroding fiscal space.
- Blanket cuts cannot solve the problem: they typically squeeze investment and public services, lowering future growth and worsening the subsidy spiral.
- Activating labour reserves - moving one million women from part-time to full-time work, employing one million benefit recipients and extending one million pensioners' working lives - could preserve a 14-percent discretionary share by 2035.
- Even with successful growth-oriented reforms, the existing debt brake framework is too tight; a structural reform is unavoidable to prevent a US-style permanent emergency budgeting.

## Schlussfolgerungen
- Realign budget planning around the long-term objectives of high employment, sustainable growth and rising incomes rather than across-the-board cuts.
- Conduct rapid interministerial spending reviews on innovation and green/digital transition, labour incentives and transfers, public services, and infrastructure development (see also [Wie wir die Modernisierung Deutschlands finanzieren](/wiki/publikationen/modernisierung-deutschlands-finanzieren)).
- Reform the debt brake to permit sustainable but rule-bound fiscal policy (see [Eine Agenda für die Expertenkommission zur Schuldenbremse](/wiki/publikationen/eine-agenda-fuer-die-expertenkommission-zur-schuldenbremse)).
