## Kernthesen
- The Commission's reform orientations interpret fiscal sustainability as convergence to a 60% debt-to-GDP ratio, effectively pursuing a shrinking of the public balance sheet.
- This goal is likely incompatible with the required ramp-up in defence, secure energy supply, and a competitive green economy; EU-aggregate debt-to-GDP would likely rise significantly above its 2020 peak of 92% under such investment needs.
- Debt Sustainability Analyses (DSAs) are problematic as central instrument of multi-year fiscal paths because debt projections are highly uncertain — Italy's debt is projected between 126% and 168% within five years with 80% probability.
- DSAs still depend on unobservable variables like potential output and growth, undermining transparency, ownership, and predictability — concerns the Commission itself has voiced.
- DSAs risk creating self-fulfilling prophecies: projections of rising debt raise financing costs, while projections of low growth justify tight fiscal balances that suppress demand and growth.

## Schlussfolgerungen
- DSAs should not be used as apolitical algorithms to define optimal fiscal policy but as analytical tools showing likely debt dynamics under specific assumptions.
- A DSA could calibrate a common fiscal benchmark (e.g. primary fiscal stance) for all countries based on agreed assumptions, with exemptions for low-debt countries or those with significant slack — see .
- The fundamental conflict between shrinking balance sheets and strengthening European sovereignty cannot be resolved by even the most intricate fiscal rules — investment needs in defence, energy, and green industries require sustained public spending.
