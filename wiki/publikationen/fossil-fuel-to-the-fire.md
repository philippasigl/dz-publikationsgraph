## Kernthesen
- Energy made an outsized contribution to the recent inflationary wave in Europe: on average 50 percent of year-on-year inflation in 2022 was directly due to energy, the vast majority of which stemmed from fossil fuel price rises.
- Fossil fuel price volatility is the main driver of energy-price volatility, with liquid fuels and gas exhibiting by far the largest swings; gas-fired power plants transmit these spikes into electricity prices.
- Faster renewable energy deployment between 2021 and 2023 saved European consumers around 95 billion euro (cumulative through end of 2023) and reduced electricity prices by 8 percent (2022) and 15 percent (2023).
- Over the long run, the secular downwards trend in renewable energy costs implies further savings and, once the transition is complete, structurally greater price stability.
- The mid-transition phase is more complex: gas-linked electricity pricing, supply-chain bottlenecks for renewables, and underinvestment in grids and storage could create new spikes if policy does not act.

## Schlussfolgerungen
- Europe should accelerate renewable deployment and decouple electricity pricing from gas to lock in the inflation-dampening benefits of clean energy, see also [[wirtschaftliche-mehrkosten-durch-energiepreisanstiege]] and [[belastungswirkung-von-klimapolitik]].
- Policy must scale up investment in grids, energy storage, supply- and demand-side flexibility – the IEA estimates global grid investment must roughly double from around 300 billion dollars to over 600 billion per year.
- Supply chains for renewable technologies should be actively monitored to avoid bottlenecks and geopolitical concentration risks as deployment accelerates.
